THE IMPORTANCE OF SOCIAL WELLBEING

Unlike team sports or activities where members gather together to practice and refine their skill, musicians spend a lot of time alone, practicing the same piece, the same phrase, the same bar, over and over again. All this time spent alone can negatively affect a musician’s social wellbeing. But what is social wellbeing and why is it so important?

TOGETHER EVEN WHEN FAR APART

Also known as social wellness, social wellbeing refers to a person’s relationships and how they interact with others (UCDAVIS, 2020). A person with a healthy social wellbeing is able to perform social roles effectively and comfortably, and create a supportive network (UCDAVIS, 2020). Social wellbeing involves building supportive and healthy relationships as well as fostering a genuine connection with the people around you (UCDAVIS, 2020). 

While the balance between a social life and an academic or professional life is different for everyone and may involve a lot of trial and error, maintaining an optimal level of social wellness allows us to build both healthy relationships and a supportive social network (UCDAVIS, 2020). These connections allow us to develop assertive skills and become comfortable with who we are in social situations (UCDAVIS, 2020). Social wellbeing is important as it enables us to create boundaries that encourage communication, trust and conflict management (UCDAVIS, 2020). Positive social wellness is crucial to building emotional resilience and we can increase our self-esteem by surrounding ourselves with positive social networks (UCDAVIS, 2020).

My Survey

I conducted a survey asking musicians in my community about social wellbeing and their participation in community music groups. I surveyed 22 individual musicians who, at the time of the survey, participated in community groups and ensembles, including school-based groups.
The data that was collected reflects the points made on this page, highlighting that a musician’s social wellbeing is positively impacted by their participation in musical ensembles and groups.
