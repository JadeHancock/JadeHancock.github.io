THE BENEFITS OF PARTICIPATION IN COMMUNITY MUSIC ENSEMBLES

So, we’ve addressed why both music and social wellbeing are not only important aspects of a musician’s life but also the lives of everyday people who may simply listen to music for enjoyment. But how can we combine both the benefits of music and improve our social wellbeing? As hinted in the previous page, one way to get the best of both worlds would be to participate in a local community band or ensemble.

Why Choose Ensembles

What it is about playing with other people that we enjoy so much?
And why is this such a great thing?

1

The Happy Hormone

When people make music together, endorphins are released which relieves the participants of stress and pain and generally makes them feel better and happier (Suttie, 2015).

2

Our Belonging

Music helps people to participate in social activities, develop a sense of place and belonging and overall feel more accepted within their community (Packer & Ballantyne, 2011).

3

Character Development

By engaging in musical activities within a community setting, participants can develop their own character, gain more confidence and create a higher self-esteem (MacDonald, 2013).

To what extent is the social wellbeing of a musician impacted by the participation in community ensembles compared to solo musicians who do not participate in community music ensembles?

Community music can be described as an influential approach to both music and music education (MacDonald, 2013). Musical groups who present shows and performances within a community are known to help expose the community to various musical activities (MacDonald, 2013). Community members are also more likely to engage with each other when exposed to such artistic activity (Plimmer, 2018). Music within a community helps to ‘bind communities’ (MacDonald, 2013) and social cohesion is seen at higher levels when young people listen to or participate in music with family members or peers (Suttie, 2015).

A key factor in our decisions to engage in community music making are our musical tastes and listening habits (MacDonald, 2013). An example of this would be that a musician who enjoys listening to jazz or Big Band music may find themselves playing an instrument in bands that perform these genres of music. An individual who enjoys singing and has their own religious beliefs may choose to participate in a choir or singing ensemble of their local religious group. However, it may not just be the instrument we play or the music we listen to that influences our participation in community music. The Homeless Choir was established in 1996 to help homeless men alleviate their depression and engage in social activities (Clift & Hancox, 2006). The members of the choir believed that singing restored their mental and physical balance, helped them to gain emotional stability and improved their social interaction skills (Clift & Hancox, 2006).

Playing in groups and community ensembles has been linked to higher self-esteem, motivation and self-efficiency in musicians and also improves self-image and self-awareness which in turn creates positive self-attitudes (Hallam, 2010).


Participating in musical groups helps individuals to make friends and learn to bond with peers in a shared activity or goal (Compton-Dickinson, 2017).



Ensemble music increases contact and coordination with other players as individuals try to ‘synch’ musically with their fellow musicians (Suttie, 2015).
