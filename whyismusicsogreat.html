WHY IS MUSIC SO GREAT?

Before we get into the social benefits of musical groups and ensembles, lets discuss the benefits of simply playing or listening to music as an individual.

Academic Benefits

- Beneficial to child developmemt
- Improved motor and speech skills
- Additional learinng 

  • As a musician myself I may be slightly biased when I say that music is an important academic subject taught in schools all over the world. Not only is music important for a child’s brain development, it is also crucial for an increase in human contact and even stress release (Wilson, 2020). A 2008 study investigating the association between instrumental music training in childhood and outcomes related to and distantly related to music found that children who had received at least three years of instrumental training generally outperformed control counterparts (Forgeard, et al., 2008). Children with two years of piano training were shown to have improved significantly on previous motor proficiency tests compared to controls (Forgeard, et al., 2008). The American study also highlighted that music training may strengthen verbal skills (Forgeard, et al., 2008) in young children and as music lessons functioned as additional schooling, children who participated in additional music training were found to have increases to; focused attention, memorisation and mastery of technical skills (Forgeard, et al., 2008).
  • Outside of the classroom, music has been found to have both physical and mental benefits as many areas of the brain becomes ‘activated’ or ‘strengthened’ when someone makes or listens to music (Suttie, 2015). In a 2010 study, lower mortality rates were found in those who made music or sang in choirs (Hallam, 2010). Music also aids physical fitness as the study found that in adults, playing the piano exercises the heart as much as a brisk walk would (Hallam, 2010). Music can help alleviate the effects of some disorders (Philippe, et al., 2019) and has been shown to help stave off or slow dementia (Plimmer, 2018). Patients who suffered with schizophrenia were also found have improved mental organisation after participating in musical therapy (Compton-Dickinson, 2017).
